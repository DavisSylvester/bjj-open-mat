# Tatami Cartography

**Philosophy:** The mat is a map. Every open session is a coordinate on an invisible grid that
spans cities, gyms, and time zones — and the practitioner is a cartographer of the roll,
plotting the next place to train. This movement treats the humble tatami weave as a
survey instrument: a rectilinear lattice against which presence, distance, and belonging are
measured. It is athletic and clinical at once — the discipline of sport rendered with the
restraint of a field diagram.

**Space and form.** Composition is built on a strict orthogonal grid, an homage to both the
tatami's woven squares and the surveyor's map graticule. Lines are thin, exact, and patient —
laid down as though by a hand that has drawn ten thousand of them. Large zones of quiet
negative space let a single dominant form breathe; the eye is guided, never crowded. Every
alignment is the product of countless refinements, each margin measured to the pixel.

**Color and material.** A limited, cohesive palette: deep slate near-black as the ground,
a warm apricot as the single voice of energy, and bone-white for the essential marks. Apricot
is used sparingly and with intent — a pin dropped on a map, a light switched on, the signal
that says *here, now, alive*. The restraint is the craftsmanship; nothing decorative, everything
deliberate, master-level in its economy.

**Scale and rhythm.** Typography behaves like signage on a mat's edge — condensed, upright,
confident. One monumental typographic gesture anchors the field; around it, tiny monospaced
labels act as reference markers and coordinates, the clinical annotations of an imaginary
discipline devoted to studying where grapplers gather. The contrast between the giant and the
whisper creates tension and reward for sustained viewing.

**Composition and hierarchy.** Information is encoded spatially: what is *available* is solid,
lit, and forward; what is *coming* is outlined, patient, held in reserve. Status is shown, not
explained. The whole is meticulously crafted to feel like an artifact from a design studio at
the top of its field — a poster that took painstaking hours, where the tatami grid, the map pin,
and the belt stripe are woven so quietly into the structure that only a grappler feels them,
while anyone sees a masterful composition.
